**Correction:** Defensible version: "Okabe-Ito's hex values are a poor fit for this app — it was built for figures and print on light grounds, uses chroma 0.117-0.172 (roughly 2-3.4x our 0.050-0.065 budget), spans OKLCH L 0.532-0.902, and includes #F0E442, a light yellow at L=0.902. Steal the method (stagger lightness, verify under CVD simulation), not the hexes."

Four corrections are required. (1) Drop the Brown 1952 field-size argument or reverse it: a 76dp tile subtends only ~2.3 degrees at 30cm viewing distance, which is Brown's SMALL-field regime where discrimination is reduced and surround-dependent — reaching his favorable 12-degree condition needs ~397dp. Brown, applied to the real geometry, argues for MORE chroma separation on a dark surround, not less. (2) "5-10x the muted budget" should be "roughly 2-3.4x". (3) Do not claim Okabe-Ito was "designed for thin lines and small markers." Per jfly.uni-koeln.de/color/, its vividness criterion was for color-NAME identifiability and screen/print fidelity, and Okabe & Ito explicitly advise making objects "as thick or big as possible." Nameability plausibly matters MORE in AAC, so this reason does not dissolve at 76dp. (4) Do not cite aesthetic-preference data against bright yellow or for muted chroma: Palmer & Schloss find people prefer MORE saturated colors over less, and the preference minimum is DARK yellow/olive, not the light yellow #F0E442 is. A weaker anti-yellow argument survives on hue (a yellow/chartreuse preference trough), not luminance. The stronger available argument for muted tiles — uncited by the claim — is Palmer & Schloss's finding that saturated colors are the LEAST preferred for colored OBJECTS, as opposed to context-free color squares.

Also: cite the palette as 8 colors (including #000000) or state you are scoring the 7 chromatic ones; drop the Paul Tol citation (dead host; his technical note is silent on mark-size vs. chroma and never mentions Okabe-Ito); and describe Grandgeorge & Masataka 2016 accurately as one small unreplicated study (29 ASD boys, 38 TD boys, boys only) that tested six named colors and never manipulated luminance, whose luminance mechanism is author speculation.

Most important for the design decision: retire "would actively harm it." On the researcher's own cat2.py metric, Okabe-Ito (protan 9.1 / deutan 8.0 / tritan 6.8) outperforms the proposed staggered palette (protan 5.6 / deutan 5.6 / tritan 4.7) on every colorblind axis. Rejecting OI is a defensible aesthetic and context-fit call; it should be made with the knowledge that it costs real CVD discriminability, and that gap deserves to be closed rather than obscured.

**Evidence:** The ARITHMETIC IS CLEAN — unusually so. The CVD sim is Vienot 1999 on linear sRGB, a legitimate method. Both hard citations are real and, at the abstract level, accurately described: JOSA josa-42-11-837 is genuinely W.R.J. Brown (1952), "The Effect of Field Size and Chromatic Surroundings on Color Discrimination," and it genuinely reports 12° vs 2° matching fields, better discrimination at 12°, and surround-independence at 12° vs surround-dependence at 2°. PubMed 3985906 is Ward, Greene & Martin (1985), Aviat Space Environ Med 56(3):251-253, "Color discrimination as a function of saturation, field size, and adaptation level" — real and on-point.

But FOUR LOAD-BEARING SUPPORTS FAIL, three of them inverting the claim's own logic.

(1) THE BROWN 1952 INFERENCE IS APPLIED BACKWARDS — this is the decisive one. Android dp is density-independent: 76dp = 76/160 inch = 1.21 cm. At a 30 cm phone viewing distance that subtends 2.30° (1.97° at 35cm, 2.76° at 25cm). That is Brown's SMALL-field condition — the 2° foveal case where he found discrimination is REDUCED and surround-DEPENDENT. It is not remotely the 12° case. To reach 12° at 30cm a tile must be 6.3 cm ≈ 397dp. So the sentence "large field size lowers chromatic discrimination thresholds... so 76dp+ tiles need far less chroma" invokes a result that does not apply to the actual geometry, and Brown read correctly at 2.3° argues the opposite: you need MORE chroma separation, and the surround (here a dark #171411) matters. The weaker relative claim survives (2.3° >> a ~0.1° scatter dot), but the magnitude "far less chroma" gets no support from Brown.

(2) "5–10× THE MUTED BUDGET" IS INFLATED ~3×. The proposed palette (cat2.py TEST B/C) uses C = 0.050/0.055/0.065. Against it, OI's 0.117–0.172 is 1.8×–3.4×. Even reading the budget as generously as the widest sweep in cat.py (C=0.030), the range is 2.0×–5.7×. Nothing in the corpus yields 10×, and 5× requires cherry-picking OI's most chromatic color against a chroma the proposal doesn't use.

(3) OKABE-ITO'S DESIGN RATIONALE IS MISSTATED, AND THE REAL ONE SURVIVES THE REBUTTAL. Primary source (jfly.uni-koeln.de/color/) states three criteria: unambiguous to colorblind and non-colorblind viewers; "vivid colors so that color names are easy to identify"; and printable with similar color on screen and in print. The vividness is for COLOR-NAME IDENTIFIABILITY and print fidelity — not "tiny marks must pop." Worse, Okabe & Ito explicitly advise the OPPOSITE of designing for small marks: "Make texts and objects as thick or big as possible. When the size of color-coded objects is small, only a few cone cells can be used for recognition," and they warn thin lines in blue/yellow are hard to read. The palette targets micrographs, DNA chips, graphs, presentations and documents — not specifically thin lines on white. This matters because nameability is arguably MORE load-bearing in an AAC app (a caregiver saying "tap the blue one") than in a scatter plot, so the actual reason for OI's chroma is not dissolved by making tiles big.

(4) THE AESTHETIC-PREFERENCE DATA IS INVERTED. Palmer & Schloss (their own Encyclopedia of Color Science chapter, reporting the PNAS 2010 dataset) find: "people generally prefer more saturated colors over less saturated ones, with little difference between the light (pastel) and muted tones, at least in the USA." That contradicts using aesthetic-preference data to justify a muted budget. And the preference MINIMUM is at DARK yellow (olive/greenish brown), NOT bright yellow — "dramatic decreases in preference for dark orange (brown) and particularly for dark yellow... relative to the light and muted oranges and yellows." #F0E442 at L=0.902 is a LIGHT yellow, i.e. the variant that is rescued, not the one that craters. So "highest-luminance stimulus ... is exactly what the aesthetic-preference data point away from" gets the mechanism exactly backwards. A weaker anti-yellow point does survive on the HUE axis (a preference trough around yellow/chartreuse; #F0E442 sits at H=105), but that is hue, not luminance. One nuance genuinely helps the claim and it wasn't cited: the same source notes saturated colors "are actually the least preferred colors for all of the objects tested" — context-free color squares behave differently from colored objects.

ALSO: (a) The Paul Tol citation is padding — personal.sron.nl does not resolve (live page is sronpersonalpages.nl/~pault/), and Tol's technical note SRON/EPS/TN/09-002 contains no discussion of mark-size vs. required chroma and never mentions Okabe-Ito. It substantiates nothing here. (b) "7 colors" — the published Okabe-Ito palette is 8 entries including #000000 black; scoring the 7 chromatic ones is defensible but should be stated. (c) The autism citation is honestly labeled "(weak)" and that hedge is fair, but "luminance literature" oversells it: Grandgeorge & Masataka 2016 (Front. Psychol.) is n=29 ASD boys vs 38 TD boys, boys only, ages 4-17, testing six NAMED colors (red, pink, yellow, brown, green, blue) — it never manipulated luminance at all. The "hyper-sensitivity to luminance" explanation is the authors' post-hoc speculation, not a measured result, and it is unreplicated.

THE CONCLUSION "WOULD ACTIVELY HARM IT" IS UNSUPPORTED ON THE CLAIM'S OWN METRIC. cat2.py shows Okabe-Ito scoring protan 9.1 / deutan 8.0 / tritan 6.8, while the researcher's own proposed staggered palette (TEST C) scores protan 5.6 / deutan 5.6 / tritan 4.7 — and the 5-hue version (TEST B) is worse still (normal 6.3). Okabe-Ito beats the proposal on EVERY colorblind discriminability metric, by a wide margin, using the researcher's own scoring code. The alleged "harm" rests entirely on chroma/luminance comfort, and both pillars of that case (the Brown field-size inference and the aesthetic-preference data) fail as shown above.

WHAT SURVIVES: OI is meaningfully higher-chroma than the proposal (1.8–3.4×, verified); OI is not iso-lightness (L range 0.370, verified); OI does contain a bright yellow that is its highest-L member (verified); OI was built for figures/print on light grounds, not dark-surface touch tiles, so lifting its hexes onto #171411 is off-spec; and "steal the method — vary lightness, verify under simulation — not the hex values" is sound advice that holds regardless. The direction is defensible; the argument used to reach it is substantially wrong.
