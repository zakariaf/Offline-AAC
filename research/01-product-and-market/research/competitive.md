## Summary

The market gap claim is PARTIAL, and the weakest part is the part the MVP is built on. The proposed MVP — offline phrase-tile grid + type-to-speak + on-device TTS + no account, marketed to adults — already exists as Speech Assistant AAC (ASoft, Netherlands): $24.99 iOS one-time / free-with-IAP Android, explicitly offline ("does not require an internet connection"), explicitly marketed to adults with aphasia/ALS/stroke, 4.6 stars, customizable categories and buttons, no subscription. That is not an adjacent competitor; it is the described product, shipping, cheap, on both platforms. Second, iOS 17+ Live Speech gives type-to-speak plus saved phrases, offline after voice download, free, zero-install, on every iPhone — it solves the type-to-speak half of the core job for free on the platform premium competitors monopolize. Third, the pricing premise is partly wrong: Proloquo2Go is $249.99 (not $299), Proloquo4Text — AssistiveWare's explicitly adult, literate-user, text-based app — is $119.99, and Spoken markets itself to adults/teens on iOS+Android with a free tier. Where the claim DOES survive: no incumbent is positioned for autistic adults with *situational, episodic* speech loss (all adult AAC positions around permanent/degenerative loss — aphasia, ALS, stroke); Android has no Live Speech equivalent (Select to Speak reads the screen, it is not an AAC); and peer-reviewed 2024-2025 work (arXiv 2404.17730, 12 autistic adults) directly confirms the infantilization complaint in users' own words. The honest read: the *functional* gap is filled, the *positioning and taste* gap is real but thin, and taste is not defensible against a $24.99 incumbent.

### Peer-reviewed 2024-2025 research CONFIRMS the infantilization complaint in autistic adults' own words — this part of the thesis is solid

*Confidence: high, **LOAD-BEARING***

### Research independently validates the shutdown/distress design requirement — low-effort, no-navigation, pre-planned content

*Confidence: medium, **LOAD-BEARING***

Findings converge on: 'A complex app requiring navigation through multiple menus may be too demanding during high-anxiety mute episodes; simple, fast, low-effort options work better'; and AAC should 'allow users to preplan content on days they have more energy.' Rachel Walker (2025) frames autistic adult speech loss as resource depletion/burnout rather than behavior. Participants in the Aging Up study described changed communication ability during shutdown and the effort required to communicate then. This validates the one-handed-in-distress design risk as a REAL and under-addressed requirement — and it is the thing incumbents (built around permanent loss, where the user is calm and configuring at leisure) genuinely do not design for.

### Insurance/Medicaid funding structurally EXCLUDES consumer apps — which is why this niche is unfunded, and also why it is commercially thin

*Confidence: high, **LOAD-BEARING***

US Medicare/Medicaid/private insurers cover speech-generating devices (SGDs) as Durable Medical Equipment under medical-necessity documentation (CMS NCA CAG-00055N; SGD Policy Article A52469). But coverage is written for DEDICATED devices — 'device software/apps may be limited to only those necessary for AAC and the functioning of the device itself.' A general-purpose phone running a $25 app is not a dedicated SGD. 2025 saw incremental easing for eye-gaze/SGD approval, not app coverage. IMPLICATION CUTS BOTH WAYS: it explains why no funded vendor serves cash-paying autistic adults (no reimbursement = no salesforce = no incumbent attention — a real opening), but it also means this product can NEVER access the funding pool. Revenue is capped at what an individual disabled adult pays out of pocket — and the incumbent in that exact slot charges $24.99. Note also: situational autistic speech loss is unlikely to clear a 'medical necessity' bar that assumes permanent impairment.

## Product implications

- **[must-have-mvp]** VERDICT: the gap is PARTIAL and the functional half is already filled. Do not build or pitch this as 'no one has built the offline adult AAC.' Speech Assistant AAC is that product, at $24.99, on both platforms, offline, adult-marketed, since ~2016. Before writing code, install it and Spoken and use them through an actual shutdown. If they are good enough, the honest answer is to ship nothing — or to contribute to them.
  - The core claim was tested and did not survive. Every downstream decision depends on whether the incumbent is genuinely inadequate or merely unfashionable. That is an empirical question answerable in one hour for $24.99, and it is the highest-value hour available. Note the incumbent already offers side-of-screen button placement for one-handed use — one of the stated differentiators is already shipped.
- **[must-have-mvp]** Reposition around the ONE genuinely unserved axis: episodic/situational speech loss in people who normally speak. Every adult AAC incumbent assumes permanent or degenerative loss (aphasia, ALS, stroke) — a user who configures calmly and uses it always. Nobody designs for a user who was speaking 10 minutes ago, is now in shutdown, and needs zero-navigation access under distress.
  - This is the only differentiator that survived adversarial search. It is confirmed by research ('a complex app requiring navigation through multiple menus may be too demanding during high-anxiety mute episodes'; 'preplan content on days they have more energy') and it implies a genuinely different product: calm-state authoring mode vs distress-state one-tap mode, no menus, no configuration surface at point of use. Feature parity with Speech Assistant AAC is not the goal — this split is.
- **[must-have-mvp]** Ship Android first, or Android-only. Drop the iOS-parity assumption from the MVP.
  - On iOS you compete against free, preinstalled, offline Live Speech with saved Favorites — which already does type-to-speak — plus Proloquo4Text at $119.99 and Personal Voice for on-device neural TTS. On Android there is NO built-in type-to-speak at all, and Proloquo2Go/TouchChat/LAMP are all absent. The platform asymmetry is the most defensible structural fact found. Flutter makes this a sequencing decision, not an architecture one — take the free option.
- **[must-have-mvp]** Make offline a HARD, verifiable, marketed guarantee — airplane-mode-provable, no network permission in the manifest at all. Attack Spoken specifically on this.
  - Spoken is the most adult-positioned competitor (300k users, iOS+Android, free tier) and its site does NOT state offline capability anywhere, while advertising AI word prediction and next-gen TTS — strongly implying cloud dependency. CoughDrop is cloud-first with account gating. Shipping an app with literally no network permission is a claim no competitor can match without re-architecting, and it is instantly verifiable by a skeptical disabled user — which is the trust currency in this community.
- **[should-have-v1]** Solve nonbinary / middle-pitch offline voice. Treat it as a headline feature, not a setting.
  - The Aging Up study (12 autistic adults) surfaced missing nonbinary/middle-pitch voices as a specific unmet need, and gender-diverse voices as a named future research direction. The autistic adult population overlaps heavily with the trans/nonbinary population. No incumbent serves this — Proloquo4Text ships 150 Acapela voices and Spoken offers a feminine-to-masculine slider, neither of which is the same as a designed nonbinary voice. This is a sharper, more defensible, more emotionally load-bearing wedge than 'dark mode and better typography.'
- **[must-have-mvp]** Prototype flutter_tts output on mid-range Android BEFORE building any UI, and set a kill criterion: if default Android TTS sounds worse than Speech Assistant AAC, the project does not proceed as specified.
  - Stated risk #1 was 'TTS must sound acceptable,' and research confirms voice quality is an active user complaint across the category. Incumbents solved it by licensing Acapela/ElevenLabs or leaning on Apple Personal Voice — all paths the offline constraint or the Android choice closes off. This is the constraint most likely to kill the product and the cheapest one to test first. Test the thing that can kill you before you build the thing that can't save you.
- **[explicitly-avoid]** Kill the paid-app revenue model. Assume near-zero revenue and price free or nominal; if money is needed, it does not come from this audience.
  - Insurance/Medicaid/CMS fund DEDICATED speech-generating devices as DME, not consumer apps — so there is no reimbursement path, and episodic autistic speech loss is unlikely to clear a medical-necessity bar built for permanent impairment. 10 of 12 study participants named cost as a barrier and gravitate to free apps. The functional incumbent charges $24.99 and has a free Android tier; Live Speech is free. A premium price against a free preinstalled OS feature and a $24.99 loved incumbent is not viable. Build this because it should exist, not as a business.
- **[explicitly-avoid]** Do not build symbol grids, word-frequency tracking, progress analytics, caregiver accounts, or any therapy/clinical framing — ever.
  - Participants explicitly asked to ELIMINATE word-frequency tracking because it reads as child-focused SLP surveillance, and named symbol-first design as the infantilizing element. These are the features that would make the product resemble what the audience is fleeing. This is a rare case where a competitor's feature list is a list of things NOT to build — and where restraint is the product.
- **[must-have-mvp]** Validate with r/autism, r/AutisticAdults and r/selectivemutism BEFORE building — ask specifically why Speech Assistant AAC, Spoken, and Live Speech Favorites are not already enough.
  - Reach is genuinely strong and the community is concentrated and word-of-mouth-driven, but the literature notes autistic adults often route around AAC entirely via texting, notes apps, and written cards — the Aging Up participants said so directly. The 30% situational-mutism figure is lifetime-episodic, not 'will adopt an AAC app.' If the answer to 'why isn't Speech Assistant AAC enough?' is vague or aesthetic, the demand is not there. Ask the question in a form that can come back 'no.'
