# Who Reed is for, and what to call them

Reed serves people whose speech comes and goes. Getting the words for that right is not etiquette — wrong vocabulary produces wrong features, wrong copy, and a wrong roadmap. This skill fixes the terms, the segment, the cuts, and the confidence level.

## The clinical terms of art — use them verbatim

The established terms are **part-time AAC use**, **unreliable speech**, and **intermittent speech**. The field's tripartite split:

| Term | Means | In Reed's terms |
|---|---|---|
| **intermittent** | "I can talk, but only sometimes" | Reed's segment. Speech is intact, *access* to it fails episodically. |
| **unreliable** | May say things that do not match the intended meaning | Also served. Speech happens but misrepresents. |
| **insufficient** | Speaks accurately, but not sufficiently | Also served. Fluent-sounding, still cannot convey need. |

Use these words in the store listing, onboarding, and any community post. They cost nothing and buy credibility with both users and referring clinicians, who recognise them instantly. Generic phrasing ("people who struggle to talk") signals an outsider.

Adopt the field's own framing: **"AAC is not the backup plan; it is the plan."** Never write copy implying AAC is what you fall back on when speech fails — that is the framing this population is actively fighting.

**Never write these:**

| Wrong | Why | Right |
|---|---|---|
| "loses their voice" / "goes mute" | Speech loss ≠ voice loss; "mute" reads as slur to this community | "goes non-speaking" / "loses access to speech" |
| "non-verbal" | Language is intact; only speech is unavailable | "non-speaking" |
| "backup for when speech fails" | Contradicts "AAC is the plan" | "a way to talk when talking is not available" |
| "suffers from" / "afflicted" | Deficit framing | "autistic adults who…" |
| "person with autism" | This community overwhelmingly prefers identity-first | "autistic person" |
| "Every mainstream AAC app is built for young children" | Verifiably false and will be fact-checked in public | "Every mainstream *symbol-grid* app is built for children" |

That last row matters more than it looks: an adult-first text AAC product has existed and been marketed to literate adults for over a decade. Overclaiming novelty in front of an audience that fact-checks is unrecoverable. Positioning must survive someone who has used the competition.

## The v1 segment: autistic adults with shutdown — and nobody else

One segment. It is chosen because the profile is coherent: literate, intact language, intermittent *access* failure, reachable community, and — critically — **they speak most days**, which is what makes recording your own voice per tile viable at all.

Four populations were considered and three were cut. They are four different products sharing one insight about a *state*. Do not re-add them because a request sounds sympathetic.

| Cut | Reason to hold the line |
|---|---|
| **Selective mutism** | Anxiety-driven, not capacity-driven. The clinical goal is often *not to draw attention* — so a device that announces disability is the opposite accommodation. It manifests far more in children than adults, so the adult segment is small. Be honest that the evidence cuts both ways: AAC has also been used in the hope that successful communication *reduces* speech anxiety, and the loudest counter-caution is a practitioner blog, not a study. Resolution: user-selectable output, never default to speaking, don't claim to know. |
| **Aphasia** | It **inverts every assumption**. Anomia and alexia commonly co-occur, so a type-to-speak box may be the *least* accessible modality for that group. Their evidence base is personalised photo Visual Scene Displays, not grids — adults with aphasia identify VSD themes more rapidly and with fewer visual fixations than on grid displays. Claiming aphasia attracts users Reed cannot serve and invites clinical criticism it cannot answer. Photo tiles are a crude partial bridge at best; never a positioning claim. |
| **Post-seizure** | Postictal confusion means **no navigation is possible at all**. That user needs one button or a lock-screen card, not a 3×4 grid. Shipping a grid to them is shipping nothing. |

When a request arrives to "also support X": ask whether X's evidence base recommends grids and typing. If it recommends photo scenes, single buttons, or partner-mediated setup, X is a different product. Say so, in those words.

## What part-time users actually need

These are the facts that overturn the obvious design instincts. Each one has a decision attached.

- **Part-time use is the dominant pattern, not a doomed one.** Of the twelve adults in the only study of this population, only two were full-time AAC users; ten persisted as part-time users. Do not treat part-time use as churn-in-progress or design a funnel toward full-time adoption.
- **They pre-program whole phrases in a capacity window.** Ten of twelve do this, one explaining *"I can pre-program phrases before I enter a situation."* Pre-programmed phrases are the **remedy** for intermittent use, not its casualty. This is the single strongest validation the product has. Protect the authoring path accordingly, and let the crisis-state self teach the calm-state self rather than requiring prediction.
- **The real barrier is social fear and masking, not motor retrieval.** *"I have a communication book that I carry with me, but I've not been brave enough to use it."* Eleven of twelve use AAC only in environments they perceive as safe; four avoid social situations entirely to dodge reactions to their device. Concealability — being able to communicate without visibly disclosing, e.g. looking like you are just texting — is a feature. A conspicuous, authoritative-looking disclosure surface is an anti-feature: it performs exactly the disclosure users fear, in a more permanent form.
- **They arrive carrying permission problems, not feature problems.** *"My speech was exhausting, and didn't feel natural. I did it because it was the only thing people would respond to."* *"Everyone else pushed for speech."* *"Just because I can say some of the things and even sound fluent, that doesn't mean I can tell you what I need."* Copy that legitimises part-time use costs only words. Write it.
- **Voice is identity, and this population skews trans/nonbinary.** Four of twelve — "many transgender or nonbinary" — criticised missing nonbinary/middle-pitch voices: *"Having the voice that matches every other person who uses AAC is very disempowering."* This is not fringe here. It forbids an "adult-sounding default": one participant *deliberately prefers* pitched-down child/teen TTS because of age dysphoria. Ship free, offline, user-selectable voices with pitch control — never impose one. And do not oversell pitch: f0 alone does not set perceived gender; formants and resonance do.
- **Phones, not tablets.** *"I don't take my iPad with me most of the time. It was expensive, and I don't want to break it."* Situational loss is unplanned; only the phone is present. Never design for a tablet-sized canvas or a device the user "brings."
- **Cost-constrained, but not destitute.** Ten of twelve discussed affordability; five avoided full-price apps; four gravitate to free. Never cite the "75–85% unemployed" figures — those describe autistic adults using state developmental-disability services, roughly the opposite subgroup from a literate, late-diagnosed, part-time AAC user. Peer-reviewed estimates for autistic adults generally run ~40%.
- **Infantilisation is real but not the diagnosis.** Ranked by count in the same study: typing 12/12, customisation 12/12, speed 11/12, trust/privacy 11/12, TTS quality 10/12, infantilising design 5/12. Infantilisation is the emotional core of the positioning and the fifth-ranked complaint. Lead with it in tone; never let it outrank retrieval speed, typing, customisation, privacy, or voice quality in the roadmap.
- **Privacy was validated; offline was not.** Eleven of twelve raised unauthorised data-collection concerns. Nobody in any source lost access for lack of signal. Offline is table stakes — the free competition is already offline — and it is a consequence of the privacy stance, not the wedge. Do not write "offline is essential because you might have no signal in an ER"; write the privacy claim, and write it honestly.

## Evidence versus assumption — be scrupulous

The entire evidence base is **one small, self-selected, unrefereed preprint (n=12, all US, recruited via social media)**, plus a second unrefereed n=5 asynchronous text focus group by a different team. Combined N = 17. Multiple parts of the surrounding analysis describe these as independent corroboration. **They are the same twelve people.** Whenever a claim appears to be confirmed from several directions, check whether it traces back to that one sample. It usually does.

Consequences for how to speak and decide:

- **Say "12 of 12" or "10 of 12", never "most users" or "research shows."** The precision is the honesty. A reader who checks will find exactly what was claimed.
- **Never present a count as a rate.** Twelve self-selected people are not a population estimate.
- **These are known unknowns; treat them as open, not settled:** whether reading degrades during shutdown (no source supports it — motor immobilisation and decision-making impairment *are* documented, reading is an extrapolation); whether the app-launch barrier is real (no direct testimony exists, and the counter-hypothesis is "the phone is already unlocked and in my hand"); motor precision during shutdown (unmeasured; design conservatively and say so rather than citing a threshold that does not exist); whether users want it to speak or to show; and **what the actual phrases are** — a validated adult phrase list does not exist in public sources. The only directly attested one-tap messages anywhere are *"too loud," "I need a break," "I want to go."* Building a starter set from assumption is the highest-risk shortcut in the project; tag provenance on every starter phrase so a presumption becomes a gift.
- **Do not manufacture mechanisms.** "TTS worsens shutdown by injecting audio into an overloaded channel" is stated by no source; the only audio complaint in the data is that devices are too *quiet*. "There is no stigma against writing" is one paper's hedged speculation about a single participant. Both have been repeated as if findings. Do not repeat them.
- **Abandonment base rates do not transfer.** The ~30% figure covers assistive technology generally (mobility aids worst); the ~39%-at-one-year figure surveys clinicians about *prescribed* devices, whose abandonment drivers — team failure, training gaps, funding, no user buy-in — are precisely what a free, self-selected, no-account app avoids.

## Positionality is a required field

The field's own position, published by AAC users and researchers together, is that people who use AAC must be leaders and co-creators in anything about them, and it names **tokenism** as a specific risk. So: either state plainly in the listing who built this and why, or recruit and **pay** an advisory group. Silence reads as exactly the thing that paper is about. Do not write copy in the voice of a lived-experience insider unless that is true.
